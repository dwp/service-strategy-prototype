Applying for both New Style Jobseeker's Allowance (JSA) and Universal Credit

Who can get it

You might be able to get both New Style JSA and Universal Credit if:

  • you live in the UK and have a right to work in the UK
  • you're 18 or over (there are some exceptions if you’re 16 or 17 - contact Jobcentre Plus for advice)
  • you’re under State Pension age
  • you’re not in full-time education
  • you’re not working at the moment, or you're working less than 16 hours per week on average, and are available for work
  • you do not have an illness or disability which stops you from working
  • you and your partner have £16,000 or less in savings between you
  • you have worked as an employee and paid Class 1 National Insurance contributions, usually in the last 2 to 3 years – National Insurance credits can also count

If you’re getting tax credits

If you get tax credits, they will stop if you or your partner makes a claim for Universal Credit, even if the claim is not approved. You will not be able to make a new claim for tax credits.

If your application for Universal Credit is approved, you can get an advance to help you while you wait for your first payment.

How New Style JSA and Universal Credit affect each other

Money

If both claims are approved, the money you get from New Style JSA might affect how much you get from Universal Credit.

National Insurance credits

While you’re getting New Style JSA, you’ll be awarded Class 1 National Insurance credits. These can help towards your State Pension and other contributory benefits in the future.

If you’re only getting Universal Credit, you’ll be awarded Class 3 National Insurance credits. These only help towards your State Pension.

How to apply

You'll need to apply for each benefit separately. Apply for New Style JSA first. At the end of the application, you’ll be directed to Universal Credit.

There’s a different process in Northern Ireland.

You’ll need your:

  • National Insurance number
  • bank or building society account details (or those of a family member or trusted friend)
  • employment details for the past 6 months, including employer contact details and dates you worked with them
  • private pension or annuity statement letter if you get, or could get, payments from one

It usually takes 20 minutes to complete the form. You will not be able to save your answers and come back to the form later. But you'll get a chance to check and update your answers before you send it.

The Department for Work and Pensions will treat your personal information carefully. We may use it for any of our purposes. To learn about your information rights and how we use information, see our personal information charter.
